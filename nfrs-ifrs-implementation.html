NFRS/IFRS for SMEs

Simplified framework for small and medium-sized enterprises that do not have public accountability.

Applicable To:

  • Small and medium retail businesses
  • Logistics and transportation companies
  • Technology startups and IT companies
  • Small hotels and restaurants
  • Small healthcare providers
  • Educational institutions (non-listed)

Key Features:

  • Simplified recognition and measurement
  • Reduced disclosure requirements
  • Less complex accounting treatments
  • Cost-effective implementation
  • Easier ongoing compliance

Simplified Sections:

  • Section 11: Basic financial instruments (simplified)
  • Section 12: Other financial instruments issues
  • Section 17: Property, plant and equipment (cost model only)
  • Section 18: Intangible assets (cost model only)
  • Section 19: Business combinations (simplified)
  • Section 20: Leases (operating lease approach)

First-Time Adoption (NFRS 1/IFRS 1)

Specialized framework for entities transitioning from previous GAAP (including NAS) to NFRS or IFRS for the first time.

Key Steps:

  • Step 1: Identify date of transition and prepare opening NFRS/IFRS balance sheet
  • Step 2: Select mandatory exemptions and optional exemptions
  • Step 3: Reconcile equity and profit/loss from previous GAAP to NFRS/IFRS
  • Step 4: Prepare comparative information under NFRS/IFRS
  • Step 5: Draft first-time adoption disclosures

Available Exemptions:

  • Business combinations - Not required to restate past combinations
  • Fair value as deemed cost - Use previous GAAP revaluations
  • Employee benefits - Recognize all cumulative actuarial gains/losses
  • Compound instruments - Separate components at transition date
  • Leases - Simplified transition approach for IFRS 16

Mandatory Exceptions:

  • Hedge accounting (must meet all criteria at transition)
  • Estimates (must be consistent with previous estimates)
  • Derecognition of financial assets/liabilities (prospective only)

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about NFRS/IFRS implementation

What is the difference between NFRS and IFRS?

NFRS (Nepal Financial Reporting Standards) are substantially converged with IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards). The Nepal Accounting Standards Board (NASB) has adopted IFRS with minor modifications to suit the Nepal context. Generally, if you understand IFRS, you understand NFRS.

Which entities are required to apply NFRS?

NFRS is mandatory for all listed companies, banks and financial institutions, insurance companies, and large enterprises meeting specified criteria. SMEs may apply NFRS for SMEs. The specific requirements depend on the nature and size of your organization.

How long does NFRS/IFRS implementation take?

Implementation typically takes 3-9 months depending on organization size, complexity, and current systems. Our phased approach ensures a smooth transition with minimal disruption to your operations.

What is NFRS 1 and why is it important?

NFRS 1 "First-time Adoption of Nepal Financial Reporting Standards" provides guidance for entities transitioning to NFRS for the first time. It specifies how to prepare opening NFRS balance sheet, select exemptions, and provide reconciliations. It's the starting point for all first-time adopters.

Can SMEs apply full NFRS?

Yes, SMEs can voluntarily apply full NFRS, though it's not required. However, NFRS for SMEs is specifically designed to be simpler and more cost-effective for small and medium enterprises while still providing high-quality financial information.

What ongoing support do you provide after implementation?

We provide ongoing support including assistance with complex transactions, updates on new standards, annual financial statement preparation, and staff training. Our retainer packages ensure you're always compliant and confident in your financial reporting.
